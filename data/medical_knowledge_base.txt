=== CARDIOVASCULAR DISEASES ===

HYPERTENSION (HIGH BLOOD PRESSURE)
Definition: Hypertension is a condition where the force of blood against artery walls is consistently too high. Blood pressure is measured in millimeters of mercury (mmHg) and recorded as two numbers: systolic pressure (when heart beats) over diastolic pressure (when heart rests).

Classification:
- Normal: Less than 120/80 mmHg
- Elevated: 120-129 systolic and less than 80 diastolic
- Stage 1 Hypertension: 130-139 systolic or 80-89 diastolic
- Stage 2 Hypertension: 140 or higher systolic or 90 or higher diastolic
- Hypertensive Crisis: Higher than 180/120 mmHg (requires immediate medical attention)

Symptoms: Hypertension is often called the "silent killer" because it usually has no symptoms. Some people may experience headaches, shortness of breath, or nosebleeds, but these are not specific to hypertension.

Causes and Risk Factors:
- Primary (essential) hypertension: Develops gradually with no identifiable cause
- Secondary hypertension: Caused by underlying conditions like kidney disease, thyroid problems, sleep apnea
- Risk factors: Age, family history, obesity, sedentary lifestyle, tobacco use, high sodium diet, excessive alcohol, stress, certain medications

Complications: Heart attack, stroke, heart failure, kidney damage, vision loss, metabolic syndrome, aneurysm.

Treatment:
- Lifestyle changes: DASH diet (low sodium, rich in fruits/vegetables), regular exercise (150 min/week), weight loss, limit alcohol, quit smoking
- Medications: ACE inhibitors (lisinopril), ARBs (losartan), beta-blockers (metoprolol), calcium channel blockers (amlodipine), diuretics (hydrochlorothiazide)

CORONARY ARTERY DISEASE (CAD)
Definition: CAD occurs when the major blood vessels that supply the heart become damaged or diseased, usually due to buildup of cholesterol-containing deposits (plaques) called atherosclerosis.

Symptoms:
- Chest pain (angina): Pressure, tightness, or squeezing in the chest
- Shortness of breath
- Heart attack: Severe chest pain, pain radiating to arm/jaw, sweating, nausea

Treatment:
- Lifestyle changes, medications (statins, aspirin, beta-blockers, nitrates)
- Procedures: Angioplasty with stent placement, coronary artery bypass surgery (CABG)

HEART FAILURE
Definition: Heart failure means the heart can't pump enough blood to meet the body's needs. The heart keeps working but not as efficiently as normal.

Types:
- HFrEF (reduced ejection fraction): Weakened heart muscle
- HFpEF (preserved ejection fraction): Stiff heart muscle

Symptoms: Shortness of breath, fatigue, swollen legs/ankles, rapid heartbeat, persistent cough

Treatment: ACE inhibitors, beta-blockers, diuretics, aldosterone antagonists, SGLT2 inhibitors, device therapy (ICD, CRT), heart transplant

=== DIABETES MELLITUS ===

TYPE 1 DIABETES
Definition: An autoimmune condition where the immune system attacks insulin-producing beta cells in the pancreas, resulting in little or no insulin production.

Onset: Usually in children and young adults, but can occur at any age.
Cause: Genetic and environmental factors, not related to lifestyle.

Symptoms: Increased thirst and urination, extreme hunger, weight loss, fatigue, blurred vision, frequent infections.

Treatment: Insulin therapy (multiple daily injections or insulin pump), blood glucose monitoring, carbohydrate counting, regular exercise.

TYPE 2 DIABETES
Definition: The body doesn't use insulin properly (insulin resistance) or doesn't produce enough insulin. Most common form of diabetes.

Risk Factors: Obesity, age over 45, family history, physical inactivity, prediabetes, gestational diabetes history, polycystic ovary syndrome.

Symptoms: Same as Type 1 but often milder and develop gradually. Many people have no symptoms.

Complications: Diabetic neuropathy, nephropathy, retinopathy, cardiovascular disease, foot problems, poor wound healing.

Treatment:
- Lifestyle: Weight loss, healthy diet, regular exercise (aim for A1C below 7%)
- Medications: Metformin (first-line), SGLT2 inhibitors (empagliflozin), GLP-1 agonists (semaglutide, liraglutide), sulfonylureas, insulin when needed

BLOOD GLUCOSE TARGETS:
- Fasting glucose: 80-130 mg/dL
- Post-meal (2 hours): Less than 180 mg/dL
- A1C: Less than 7% (53 mmol/mol)

=== RESPIRATORY DISEASES ===

ASTHMA
Definition: Asthma is a condition in which the airways narrow and swell and may produce extra mucus. This can make breathing difficult and trigger coughing, wheezing, and shortness of breath.

Triggers: Allergens (pollen, dust mites, pet dander), respiratory infections, exercise, cold air, smoke, air pollution, stress, certain medications (aspirin, NSAIDs, beta-blockers).

Symptoms: Shortness of breath, chest tightness, wheezing, coughing (especially at night).

Severity Classification:
- Intermittent: Symptoms less than 2 days per week
- Mild persistent: Symptoms more than 2 days per week
- Moderate persistent: Daily symptoms
- Severe persistent: Continuous symptoms throughout the day

Treatment:
- Quick-relief (rescue): Short-acting beta2-agonists (albuterol/salbutamol) — use during attacks
- Long-term control: Inhaled corticosteroids (fluticasone, budesonide), long-acting beta2-agonists (salmeterol), leukotriene modifiers (montelukast)
- Biologics for severe asthma: Omalizumab, mepolizumab, dupilumab

CHRONIC OBSTRUCTIVE PULMONARY DISEASE (COPD)
Definition: COPD is a chronic inflammatory lung disease that causes obstructed airflow from the lungs. It includes chronic bronchitis and emphysema.

Main Cause: Cigarette smoking (responsible for ~85-90% of cases); also occupational dust/chemicals, air pollution.

Symptoms: Shortness of breath (dyspnea), chronic cough with mucus, wheezing, chest tightness, frequent respiratory infections.

Diagnosis: Spirometry — FEV1/FVC ratio below 0.70 after bronchodilator.

Treatment:
- Most important: Quit smoking
- Bronchodilators: LABA (salmeterol), LAMA (tiotropium)
- Inhaled corticosteroids (when frequent exacerbations)
- Oxygen therapy for severe cases
- Pulmonary rehabilitation program
- Vaccines: Flu and pneumococcal vaccines to prevent infections

PNEUMONIA
Definition: Pneumonia is an infection that inflames the air sacs in one or both lungs, which may fill with fluid or pus.

Types:
- Community-acquired pneumonia (CAP): Most common
- Hospital-acquired (nosocomial) pneumonia
- Aspiration pneumonia
- Viral pneumonia (including COVID-19)

Symptoms: Cough with phlegm, fever, chills, shortness of breath, chest pain when breathing, fatigue.

Treatment: Antibiotics for bacterial pneumonia (amoxicillin, azithromycin, doxycycline); antivirals for viral; oxygen therapy; hospitalization for severe cases.

=== INFECTIOUS DISEASES ===

INFLUENZA (FLU)
Definition: Influenza is a contagious respiratory illness caused by influenza viruses (types A, B, C, D).

Transmission: Droplets from coughing, sneezing, or talking; contact with contaminated surfaces.

Symptoms: Sudden fever, cough, sore throat, runny nose, muscle aches, headache, fatigue. Vomiting and diarrhea more common in children.

Complications: Pneumonia, bronchitis, sinus and ear infections, worsening of chronic conditions (heart disease, asthma, diabetes).

Prevention: Annual flu vaccine (most important), handwashing, avoid touching face.

Treatment: Rest, fluids, antiviral medications (oseltamivir/Tamiflu — most effective within 48 hours of symptom onset).

COVID-19
Definition: Coronavirus disease 2019 is caused by SARS-CoV-2.

Symptoms: Range from mild to severe — fever, cough, fatigue, loss of taste/smell, shortness of breath, severe: pneumonia, ARDS, organ failure.

Prevention: Vaccination, masks, social distancing, hand hygiene.

Treatment: Supportive care; antivirals (nirmatrelvir/ritonavir — Paxlovid) for high-risk; corticosteroids (dexamethasone) for hospitalized patients needing oxygen.

Long COVID: Persistent symptoms lasting weeks to months after infection — fatigue, brain fog, shortness of breath, joint pain.

URINARY TRACT INFECTION (UTI)
Definition: UTI is an infection in any part of the urinary system — kidneys, ureters, bladder, and urethra.

Types:
- Cystitis (bladder infection): Most common
- Pyelonephritis (kidney infection): More serious

Risk Factors: Female sex (shorter urethra), sexual activity, menopause, urinary abnormalities, catheter use, diabetes.

Symptoms: Burning urination, frequent urge to urinate, cloudy/strong-smelling urine, pelvic pain (women), back/flank pain (kidney infection).

Treatment: Antibiotics (nitrofurantoin, trimethoprim/sulfamethoxazole, fosfomycin for uncomplicated UTI; fluoroquinolones for complicated).

=== MENTAL HEALTH ===

DEPRESSION (MAJOR DEPRESSIVE DISORDER)
Definition: Depression is a common and serious medical illness that negatively affects how you feel, think, and act.

Symptoms (DSM-5 criteria — 5 or more for ≥2 weeks):
- Depressed mood most of the day
- Loss of interest or pleasure (anhedonia)
- Weight change or appetite disturbance
- Sleep disturbance (insomnia or hypersomnia)
- Psychomotor agitation or retardation
- Fatigue or loss of energy
- Feelings of worthlessness or excessive guilt
- Difficulty concentrating
- Recurrent thoughts of death or suicidal ideation

Treatment:
- Psychotherapy: Cognitive-behavioral therapy (CBT), interpersonal therapy (IPT)
- Medications: SSRIs (sertraline, escitalopram — first-line), SNRIs (venlafaxine, duloxetine), bupropion, tricyclics, MAOIs
- Combination therapy most effective for moderate-severe depression
- ECT (electroconvulsive therapy) for severe/treatment-resistant cases

ANXIETY DISORDERS
Types: Generalized Anxiety Disorder (GAD), Panic Disorder, Social Anxiety Disorder, Specific Phobias, PTSD, OCD.

GAD Symptoms: Excessive worry about multiple things, restlessness, fatigue, difficulty concentrating, muscle tension, sleep problems.

Treatment: CBT (most effective), SSRIs (sertraline, escitalopram), SNRIs, buspirone, benzodiazepines (short-term only due to dependence risk).

=== GASTROINTESTINAL CONDITIONS ===

GASTROESOPHAGEAL REFLUX DISEASE (GERD)
Definition: GERD occurs when stomach acid frequently flows back into the tube connecting your mouth and stomach (esophagus).

Symptoms: Heartburn (burning in chest), regurgitation, difficulty swallowing, chest pain, chronic cough, hoarse voice.

Triggers: Large meals, lying down after eating, obesity, pregnancy, smoking, alcohol, spicy/fatty foods, coffee, citrus.

Treatment:
- Lifestyle: Lose weight, elevate head of bed, eat smaller meals, avoid triggers, don't eat 2-3 hours before bed
- Antacids (quick relief), H2 blockers (famotidine), proton pump inhibitors — PPIs (omeprazole, pantoprazole — most effective for frequent GERD)
- Surgery (Nissen fundoplication) for severe/refractory GERD

IRRITABLE BOWEL SYNDROME (IBS)
Definition: IBS is a common disorder affecting the large intestine with symptoms like cramping, abdominal pain, bloating, gas, diarrhea, and constipation.

Types: IBS-D (diarrhea predominant), IBS-C (constipation predominant), IBS-M (mixed).

Cause: Exact cause unknown. Factors: gut-brain axis dysregulation, gut microbiome changes, visceral hypersensitivity, motility disturbances.

Treatment:
- Diet: Low-FODMAP diet, avoid triggers (caffeine, alcohol, fatty foods, artificial sweeteners)
- Fiber supplements for IBS-C
- Antispasmodics (hyoscine/dicyclomine)
- Antidiarrheal (loperamide for IBS-D)
- Antidepressants (low-dose tricyclics or SSRIs) for pain
- Psychological therapies (CBT, gut-directed hypnotherapy)

=== MUSCULOSKELETAL CONDITIONS ===

OSTEOARTHRITIS (OA)
Definition: OA is the most common form of arthritis, occurring when the protective cartilage that cushions the ends of bones wears down over time.

Affected joints: Knees, hips, hands, spine.

Symptoms: Joint pain, stiffness (especially in morning but less than 30 minutes), tenderness, loss of flexibility, grating sensation, bone spurs.

Risk Factors: Age, obesity, joint injury, repetitive stress, gender (women more common), genetics.

Treatment:
- Exercise and weight loss (most important)
- Physical therapy
- Analgesics: Acetaminophen (paracetamol), NSAIDs (ibuprofen, naproxen) — topical NSAIDs preferred for knee/hand OA
- Intra-articular injections: Corticosteroids, hyaluronic acid
- Surgery: Osteotomy, joint replacement (arthroplasty) for severe cases

RHEUMATOID ARTHRITIS (RA)
Definition: RA is a chronic inflammatory disorder affecting joints, causing painful swelling that can eventually result in bone erosion and joint deformity.

Key Difference from OA: RA is autoimmune, symmetric (affects same joints on both sides), morning stiffness lasting more than 1 hour, systemic symptoms.

Symptoms: Joint pain, swelling, stiffness, fatigue, fever, weight loss.

Treatment:
- DMARDs (disease-modifying antirheumatic drugs): Methotrexate (first-line), hydroxychloroquine, sulfasalazine, leflunomide
- Biologic DMARDs: TNF inhibitors (adalimumab, etanercept), IL-6 inhibitors (tocilizumab), B-cell depletion (rituximab)
- JAK inhibitors: Tofacitinib, baricitinib
- NSAIDs and corticosteroids for symptom relief

=== COMMON MEDICATIONS ===

ANALGESICS (PAIN RELIEVERS)
- Acetaminophen (Paracetamol/Tylenol): For mild-moderate pain, fever. Max 4g/day (3g/day if elderly or liver disease). Does NOT cause GI ulcers but liver toxic in overdose.
- NSAIDs (Ibuprofen, Naproxen, Aspirin): Anti-inflammatory. Caution with peptic ulcer disease, kidney disease, cardiovascular disease, anticoagulants.
- Opioids (Morphine, Oxycodone, Tramadol): For severe pain. Risk of dependence, respiratory depression.

ANTIBIOTICS
- Amoxicillin: Broad-spectrum penicillin; ear, sinus, chest, UTI infections
- Azithromycin: Atypical pneumonia, STIs, skin infections; 5-day Z-pack
- Ciprofloxacin (fluoroquinolone): UTIs, GI infections, respiratory; avoid in children/pregnant women unless necessary
- Doxycycline: Lyme disease, chlamydia, respiratory infections, malaria prevention
- Important: Complete full antibiotic course; do not share antibiotics; antibiotic resistance is a major global health concern.

CARDIOVASCULAR MEDICATIONS
- Statins (Atorvastatin, Rosuvastatin): Lower LDL cholesterol; reduce cardiovascular events. Take at night for short-acting statins.
- Beta-blockers (Metoprolol, Carvedilol): Heart failure, hypertension, angina, arrhythmias
- ACE inhibitors (Lisinopril, Ramipril): Hypertension, heart failure, diabetic nephropathy. Can cause dry cough.
- Aspirin 81mg: Low-dose for antiplatelet therapy in established cardiovascular disease or after stroke.

=== PREVENTIVE HEALTH AND SCREENINGS ===

RECOMMENDED SCREENINGS BY AGE:
- Blood pressure: Every 1-2 years from age 18
- Cholesterol: Every 4-6 years from age 20
- Blood glucose (diabetes): Every 3 years from age 35, or earlier if risk factors
- Colorectal cancer: Colonoscopy every 10 years starting age 45
- Breast cancer (women): Mammogram every 1-2 years starting age 40-50 (guidelines vary)
- Cervical cancer (women): Pap smear every 3 years ages 21-65, or every 5 years with HPV co-test ages 30-65
- Prostate cancer (men): Discuss PSA testing with doctor at age 50 (earlier if high risk)
- Osteoporosis (women): Bone density (DEXA) scan at age 65, earlier if risk factors
- Lung cancer: Annual low-dose CT scan for heavy smokers ages 50-80

VACCINATIONS (ADULTS):
- Influenza: Annually
- COVID-19: As per current guidelines
- Tdap (tetanus, diphtheria, pertussis): Every 10 years
- Shingles (Shingrix): 2 doses for adults 50+
- Pneumococcal: PCV20 or PPSV23 for adults 65+
- HPV: Up to age 26 (or 27-45 after discussion with doctor)

LIFESTYLE RECOMMENDATIONS:
- Physical activity: 150-300 minutes moderate-intensity or 75-150 minutes vigorous-intensity per week
- Diet: Mediterranean or DASH diet — rich in fruits, vegetables, whole grains, lean proteins, healthy fats
- Sleep: 7-9 hours per night for adults
- Avoid tobacco in all forms
- Limit alcohol: No more than 1 drink/day (women) or 2 drinks/day (men); less is better
- Maintain healthy BMI: 18.5-24.9 kg/m²

=== EMERGENCY SYMPTOMS — SEEK IMMEDIATE CARE ===

Call 911 (Emergency Services) immediately for:
- Chest pain, pressure, or tightness (possible heart attack)
- Signs of stroke: Face drooping, Arm weakness, Speech difficulty, Time to call 911 (FAST)
- Severe shortness of breath
- Sudden severe headache ("thunderclap")
- Signs of severe allergic reaction (anaphylaxis): Throat swelling, difficulty breathing, severe hives after exposure to allergen
- High fever with stiff neck and photophobia (possible meningitis)
- Uncontrolled bleeding
- Loss of consciousness or altered mental status
- Suspected poisoning or overdose
